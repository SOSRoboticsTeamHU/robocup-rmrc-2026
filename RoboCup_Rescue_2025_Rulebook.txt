3
Introduction
RoboCupRescue is a family of research competitions that drive the state-of-the-science in real
world capabilities for robots used in applications such as search and rescue, hazardous
materials response, and disaster recovery. It integrates with the NIST Standard Test Methods for
Response Robots project, meaning that teams that do well in the competition have produced
systems exhibiting capabilities that are immediately interesting to the real world end users who
keep us safe every day.

This document describes the motivation, procedures, rules, and expected conduct for the
RoboCupRescue Rapidly Manufactured Robot Challenge (RMRC). It is intended to be read in
conjunction with the RMRC Arena Construction Guide. The latest versions of both this
document, and the RMRC Arena Construction Guide, are available from https://rrl-rmrc.org .


Competition Spirit
The RoboCupRescue Rapidly Manufactured Robot Challenge (RMRC) combines open,
real-world research challenges, with the rigorous, statistically significant, measurement science
focus of the Major competition. The lower cost, more structured, and pedagogical nature of the
RoboCupJunior Rescue competitions welcomes young robot researchers. It incorporates the
structure and innovation, previously only available to well funded university teams, and makes it
accessible to modestly funded high schools. The Rapidly Manufactured Robot Challenge
combines incentives for learning about critical aspects of research, such as technical
communication and collaboration.

Much like games such as golf, and as distinct from games such as chess, RMRC is a
competition where all teams work together, against the application. Teams are rewarded both for
performance against a wide variety of tests, as well as how they communicate their work to
other researchers. Instead of individual holes of golf, teams perform different standard tests
from the Standard Test Methods for Response Robots project. Unlike golf, however, teams are
free to choose which tests they perform, the order in which they do them, and the number of
times they try each test, to make sure that the result they achieve is reflective of their
capabilities.

The scoring formula rewards excellence in commonly exhibited capabilities, as well as the
demonstration of novel capabilities. Teams are further incentivised for developing novel systems
that address real-world gaps in capabilities through additional Best-in-Class award categories.




4
Who is the Competition For?
RMRC is designed for teams of up to 6 students1 and 2 mentors2 (1 mentor for 3 or fewer
students). Most teams consist of high school students, with teachers being the mentors. We
also encourage teams to form outside of schools, such as in social groups and makerspaces.
We particularly encourage university students to mentor groups of high school students, sharing
their new-found knowledge and expertise.

NOTE: In some rare cases, such as when qualified teams pull out at the last minute, we may
have enough space to admit additional team members and mentors. In general, we will only
know this within a month or two of the competition. If this is the case, an announcement will be
made on the mailing list at https://list.rrl-rmrc.org, inviting teams to apply to send more team
members and mentors. Teams with more than 6 students and 2 mentors will be expected to
make some team members available for volunteer duties at the competition.


Competition Summary
This section provides a very high level overview of how the competition is run. Detailed rules for
the different parts of the competition appear in the rest of this document. All timetables are
subject to change, please see the website and mailing list, available from https://rrl-rmrc.org for
the latest details.


Qualification
For the International competition, qualification generally involves submitting a Team
Participation Form (TPF) in response to a call for participation, and Qualification Team
Description Materials (TDMs) a month or so after that.

The Qualification TDMs are judged by the RMRC Committee to determine team’s readiness and
qualification for competition. Some spots are generally held open for teams that qualify by
winning domestic competitions and other regional qualification pathways.

Award winners of the World Championships in the previous 2 years (first, second, third, and
Best-in-Class) are automatically qualified. They must still submit the Team Participation Form by
the deadline to register their interest, and are encouraged to submit a Qualification Team
Description Paper to tell the rest of the teams what they have done since their award.

For Domestic competitions, these timings and requirements vary. Please monitor the mailing list
at https://list.rrl-rmrc.org for details and variations for any given year. Contact info@rrl-rmrc.org if
you wish to find out more information about domestic competitions that may exist in your area.


Updated Team Description Materials
Teams that qualify submit Updated Team Description Materials, due 2 weeks prior to the
competition. These materials are scored by the RMRC Committee based on how well these
materials help another team, new or otherwise, learn how to implement a particular
RMRC-relevant capability. These materials must be released open source, under a permissive
license. A growing library of these materials is available from https://rrl-rmrc.org . This score
forms a multiplier onto the team’s Preliminary Round score. This provides a strong incentive,
particularly for the best teams, to also share their knowledge and experience, allowing other
teams to build on their work.


In-person competition
The International competition itself generally consists of 1 setup day and 3.5 competition days.
Timings may vary for domestic competitions.

Setup
During the setup day, teams arrive, register, set up their robots and equipment, help to set up
the apparatuses, and practice within the apparatuses as they are completed. The first team
meeting is held with mentors and team leaders; volunteers and Judges are identified, the
timetable for the competition is announced, a safety walk-through is conducted, and any
event-specific logistics and details are also shared with the teams.

Robots are generally photographed at this point for documentation and configuration
management. As the competition separates out an overall real world mission into component
tests, the robot hardware3 should stay the same through the first 3 days of competition. For
instance, a robot that has an arm to pick up an object should also be equipped with that arm
when it traverses the terrain tests, as during a real mission it would have had to carry that arm
over the terrain to get to where it needs to pick up objects. See the Robot Configuration
definition for further details.

Preliminaries
Preliminary rounds are held during the first 2 competition days. Teams gain points by performing
the various Standard Test Methods for Response Robots within the many different, separate,
test method apparatuses that make up the arena. The precise rules and scoring methods for
each test are described in more detail later in this document.



3
 In general, software updates are ignored in recognition that these robots are prototypical and on the
premise that in a real scenario, it is conceivable that software can be changed over the air.


6
Many teams may compete in these different tests at the same time. Missions are 5 minutes long
and start every 10 minutes, with each mission confined to one test apparatus and apparatus
setting. Missions are run according to a global clock and all missions start at the same time, 6
times an hour.

This competition is team-run, meaning that all mentors are encouraged to volunteer and spend
some time as a judge. With this help from our community, we can test multiple teams
concurrently on different apparatuses. The tests are designed to be easy to score, with most
simply a matter of counting laps. There is limited-to-no need for judgment calls, the robot’s
operator double checks and verifies the tally, and the scores are written on a publicly viewable
scoreboard shortly after each mission.

Prior to each competition session, teams collaboratively determine their timetable for the
following competition period (see “At the Competition: Preliminaries” later in this document).
Given that there are so many opportunities to run, teams are not obliged to run in every time
slot, and indeed are encouraged to allow time to troubleshoot, rest, and practice.

Only the best mission score that a team achieves in a given apparatus and setting counts
towards the team’s total preliminary score, so teams have ample opportunity to repeat tests if
they choose to. The tests are designed so that within 5 minutes of robot run time, the best
teams will have ample opportunity to perform a statistically significant number of iterations of the
test. Teams do also have the opportunity to move time slots during the day, subject to
availability, in response to unexpected issues or to take advantage of opportunities to improve
performance.

The scores for each test are tabulated and normalized. For each test and setting, the best
score(s) become 100%, with all other scores normalized accordingly. This balances the scores
of easy and difficult tests, and encourages teams to develop new capabilities for tests that have
not received as much attention.

Finals
The finals are held on the 3rd day but the preparation is done on the evening of the 2nd day,
after the preliminary rounds have finished.

After the preliminary rounds, the scores are added up and combined with the updated Team
Description Material multiplier. The largest gap in the scores between the teams in 3rd to 7th
place is used as the cutoff for finals qualification, thus the number of teams admitted to the finals
can range from 3 to 6 teams. This policy acknowledges the inherent uncertainty in the scoring
process and aims to make the cut at a location where it is the most statistically significant.

Depending on the schedule, either the evening prior to the finals or the morning of the finals, the
finalist teams collaborate in designing and building the finals arena by arranging the individual
test method apparatuses into a continuous maze. Competition organizers serve to mediate
between the teams to ensure that all finalist teams have input into the design.


7
The timing of the finals day is flexible and depends on the number of qualified teams.
Depending on available time and number of finalist teams, teams run 2-4 missions of around
15-30 minutes, with 5-10 minutes between runs for take down/set up of the next team. Teams
will all have the same amount of run time available to them, robots will run one at a time, and
have access to the full maze.Teams score one point for traversing an apparatus in one direction,
and another point for traversing it in the opposite direction. Teams may traverse a given
apparatus more than once in a given direction, but receive no additional points for doing so.
Similarly, non-traversal tests, such as manipulation and sensing, can only be performed once
per mission.

Team Challenge
The last day of the competition is only a half-day, due to the award ceremony. During this time, a
casual “Team Challenge” competition is held, as a way for students, mentors, and organizers
alike to have some fun and demonstrate collaborative robot capabilities in a less stressful
manner.

Throughout the previous 3 days of the competition, the organizers solicit suggestions from
teams for games or obstacles that may be played on this last day and a decision is made on the
evening of the 2nd day, after the finalists are announced. This means that teams who did not
qualify for the finals have a day to prepare and practice. The Team Challenge varies from year
to year, based on the interests of the competitors. Unlike the other rounds, robots may be
modified for this challenge.

Awards
Awards are given for first, second, and third place, based on the number of points achieved in
the finals. Scores that are very close, generally within 5%, are considered ties, in
acknowledgement of the inherent uncertainty of performance measurement. In rare cases,
particularly if many teams are tied in the finals, organizers may use preliminary scores to break
ties. Best-in-Class awards are also given, based on a subset of preliminary scores. The RMRC
Committee may also issue an Open Source and Innovation award, and an award for the
winner(s) of the Team Challenge.


How Does a Team Get Started?
RMRC has many features that are designed to make it easy for a moderately well resourced
team to get started!


Building the Robots
Robots for RMRC are of a size that makes them easy to build using 3D printed and laser cut
parts, and with components such as controller boards, cameras, and servos that are common
and cheaply available on the hobbyist market.



8
There is a growing library of designs and tutorials, produced by teams over the past few years,
that new teams can make use of to jump-start their development. See https://rrl-rmrc.org for
details.


Building the Arena
The arena construction guide is available from https://rrl-rmrc.org and is designed to be compact
and cheap to produce. Teams may start by building a single pallet and a few different terrain
components that may be mixed and matched for testing, without the need to build a full arena.
Teams in a local region are also encouraged to get together to form larger arenas and hold
mini-competitions to practice.




9
Definitions

Apparatus
An apparatus is a piece of equipment that is used to perform one or more tests. An apparatus
may itself have variations or settings that change depending on the test.


Arena
All of the competition space, including lanes and bays. Does not include administrative areas or
the team paddock.


Bay
A single apparatus or set of apparatuses that supports one robot performing a test at a time. A
bay may support one test (possibly with multiple settings), multiple tests that share an element
(e.g., the dexterity tests which share a terrain), or multiple tests by swapping out terrains or
other apparatus elements within one bay. This may be useful for smaller regional competitions
and for teams building the apparatuses at home, where space and resources may be limited.
Bays have a marked start position.


Competition Lead
The Competition Lead(s) will keep time and overall score (on a scoreboard visible to all
competitors), without bias to any one individual or Team. They will ensure that fair play is
observed, along with managing the paperwork aspects of the competition. They will also
coordinate safety concerns (e.g. locating appropriate safety and fire equipment) and ensure
teams, mentors, and judges are kept up-to-date on announcements.


Finals
Teams who access the finals (through their in-person scores during the preliminaries, TDM
multipliers, etc), will have the opportunity to design and implement the design of the maze.

For the sake of fair play, it is strongly encouraged that all qualifying teams, who will be moving
to the Finals, work together to decide and assemble their preferred configuration as a group, on
the evening of the end of preliminaries. Once the maze has been assembled, this would allow
for the greatest amount of time to practice and familiarize themselves with the maze.


Inspection
Generally held before competition begins, the Competition Lead(s) and/or Judges will determine
if a robot configuration is safe, and follows the spirit and rules of the competition. An inspection


10
may be called for at other times, if the Competition Lead(s) and/or Judges determine there is a
need for it.


Judge
Judges are volunteered from available Mentors, (badged) Parents, and adult competitors from
the Major league. (If a Major team volunteers to assist in judging, ensure they are aware of their
own competition schedule, and count the entire team as one judge, to ensure coverage in the
event of Major chaos.) A judge will keep score, without bias, and deliver scores to the
Competition Lead for input onto the scoreboard. Judges should avoid keeping score for their
own team.


Lane
A bay that is long and thin, usually a Z-pallet (see construction guide), and is generally used for
terrain and obstacle type tests.


Lap
Some tests involve the robot performing laps of the bay.


General definition:
     1.​ Start in contact with one end wall during the mission time.
     2.​ Traverse the bay without leaving the bay.
     3.​ Make contact with the opposite end wall.
            a.​ If the robot does not make contact with the end wall before returning, both
                 (incomplete) laps do not count.
            b.​ It is up to the operator to determine if they have made contact with the end wall
                 before starting the next lap. They should not be informed of this by the judge,
                 team-mates, audience, or anyone else.
            c.​ Contact with the end walls must be made with a part of the robot (including arms,
                 wheels, and flippers). The tether or anything else connected to the robot only by
                 a wire, string, or similar does not count.


First lap:
The first lap of the mission shall begin at the end wall marked as the start position. If the robot
moves prior to the mission start time such that it is no longer in contact with the end wall, once
the mission starts it shall be driven, or positioned by hand, so that it touches the starting end
wall before continuing the mission.


Successive laps:
Successive laps shall be run in opposite directions.


11
Resets:
If a reset is called, the robot shall re-start from the starting position. See the full definition of
Reset below for more information.


Teleoperated lap:
By default, a lap is considered teleoperated. The judge will tally the lap in the teleoperated
section of the score sheet.

A robot that is teleoperated may operate wirelessly or on a tether. The operator makes
decisions based solely on the information that the robot delivers to the operator. The operator is
the only person who controls the robot's movement and manipulation capabilities.

A lap that is performed with the assistance of autonomous behaviors, such as automatically
centering in gaps, is still considered teleoperated if it does not fully satisfy the requirements of
an “Autonomous lap” (below).

In the competition setting, the operator may not look at the robot or the bay, and may not
communicate with anyone but the judge.


Autonomous lap:
For a lap to count as an autonomous lap, the operator shall not touch the controls or otherwise
issue commands to the robot unless any part of the robot, including arms, wheels, and flippers,
is within 30 cm of either end of the bay. Parts that fall off the robot and/or parts that are not
solidly attached to the robot (e.g. the tether or anything connected to the robot only by a wire,
string, or similar) do not count.

The operator may monitor the robot’s progress through the operator control unit only, without
touching it. As usual, they may not look at the robot or bay, and may not communicate with
anyone but the judge. It should be clear to the judge that they are not issuing commands to the
robot (e.g., by having their hands well clear of the OCU).

The team shall announce that they are attempting a lap autonomously prior to beginning
autonomous operation (or continuing autonomous operation in a new lap), and receive
confirmation from the judge to ensure that they are aware and can confirm that the operator is
hands-off the controls. If the team successfully completes the lap autonomously, the judge will
tally the lap in the autonomy section of the score sheet instead of the teleoperated section.

A team may attempt a lap autonomously, and during the lap, decide to take over teleoperation
outside these 30 cm areas. If they do so, the whole lap now counts as a teleoperated lap. The
judge will tally the lap in the teleoperated section of the score sheet.




12
Maze/Labyrinth
Used in the Finals, the Maze comprises the available lanes/bays, and can be configured
completely flat or tiered (with multiple levels, including the drop test), depending on the available
space and the amount of challenge the teams wish to face.

Walls, including end walls, may be removed during the configuration of the maze, in order to
integrate bays/lanes into one another.


Mentor
Each team may have up to two mentors to assist and supervise. These individuals will be
badged and are strongly encouraged to assist with running the competition (be it as a volunteer
judge, assembly of lanes, etc). They must be at least 18 years of age and be able to legally
represent the team (e.g., sign consent forms).


Mission
This is the 5 minutes of active time within a time slot, when the robot is in operation.


Multiplier
Based on a variety of factors (TDMs, autonomous operation, etc) a team’s score may be
increased. This will affect a team’s ability to move from preliminaries into the finals. Multipliers
may apply per point (e.g., reading an individual Landolt-C), or to the entire preliminary score
(e.g., the Updated TDM multiplier).


Operator Interface
The equipment, provided by the team, that the operator uses to control the robot. This includes
the operator side wireless networking equipment (e.g., the access point or the network interface,
but not including the network cable/tether).

Aisleways/thoroughfares will be kept clear of any tripping or fall hazards, including, but not
limited to, the tethers, robot/robot parts, operational equipment, etc.


Operator Station
The location where the operator controls the robot, along with the necessary equipment and
furniture (table/chair). The operator station is placed close to the lane to minimize the tripping
hazard associated with cables reaching from the operator station to the robot, but far enough so
that the operator can be positioned facing away from the lane.




13
Operators/pilots of the robots will not be able to have line of sight of the robot as the test is run.
This is to more closely duplicate the real world setting and challenges faced by rescue robots.
No-one apart from the judge shall communicate with the operator once their timed run has
begun (e.g., to indicate the robot's location, whether a test was successfully completed, etc)
apart from in an emergency, which will automatically end the mission.

Tethers may need to be run from the operator's station to the robot to allow for communications
without needing to use wireless networking. One team member may be required to manage
these lines, without disrupting the robot or pilot, and without causing a safety/tripping hazard to
others in the arena. See “Rules for Each Test” for further details on tethers.


Preliminaries
Preliminary rounds/preliminary scores are trialed first at an in-person competition. Scores from
the preliminaries will determine which teams will move onto the finals.


Penalties
All participants are expected to adhere to the intent of fair play. If this is not observed, penalties
up to, and including, dismissal from the current competition, and barring from future
competitions, may be meted out to individuals or entire teams. Further information is provided in
the Code of Conduct.


Reset and Mini-mission
If anyone physically touches/moves the robot or arena during a run (eg. robot falls out of the
terrain, robot gets stuck, tether gets pulled), a reset is called. This means that the robot is
placed at the starting position of the bay, the scores for that run reset and then the run continues
as if for a new mission, but only in the time that remains. Each ‘reset’ results in a separate
‘mini-run’ (or ‘mini-mission’ in RRL Major terminology). The judge shall record each
“mini-mission” on a new score sheet. The score of the best ‘mini-run’ becomes the score for the
whole run.


Robot Configuration
A robot configuration (often abbreviated to just “configuration” or “robot” elsewhere in this
document) refers to the robot, along with any physical accessories and settings that require
physical access to the robot to alter. A robot is expected to maintain the same robot
configuration from the start of the preliminaries to the end of the finals (or end of preliminaries if
the team does not reach the finals).




14
Motivation
The reason for this is because the competition consists of many individual tests that, together,
represent a real mission. In a real mission, a person cannot touch the robot to change things.
For instance, a robot that needs to pick up an object needs to have its arm installed as it
traverses the terrain to get to where it needs to pick up the object. Therefore, in the competition,
a robot that wishes to score points for manipulation must also have its arm installed for the
mobility tests. Software changes are generally allowed, on the premise that in a real robot, this
can often be achieved remotely.


Documentation
The configuration of the robot at the start of the competition is documented on, or prior to, the
first day, through photographs, ideally with a 20 cm grid background for scale. These
photographs can be referenced to check that the robot configuration has not changed. At
minimum, the following angles should be taken of the robot in both its Stowed Posture (see
below for definition) and a typical operating posture.
     ●​ Front, Side, and Top images.
     ●​ A “publicity” image that easily/readily identifies the robot (e.g., at an angle).
     ●​ A close-up image of unusual and special features of the robot, as declared by the team.


Changing configurations
A team may elect to change a robot configuration during the competition but this will be
considered a separate robot configuration for the purpose of scoring. Thus unless a team does
this early in the competition, they risk reducing the number of missions that they have to
accumulate a good score to qualify for the finals. The robot configuration that the team runs in
the finals must be the same as a robot configuration that accumulated a high enough score to
qualify for the finals. A team may only enter one robot configuration in the finals.


Repairs
It is understood that sometimes it is unavoidable that a robot configuration may change during
the competition, such as because the robot is damaged and it may not be possible to repair it to
exactly the same capability. If the team wishes to have the repaired robot configuration
considered to be the same as the original robot configuration, so that they can continue
accumulating scores, they shall consult with the Competition Lead(s) to see if any additional
modifications are necessary.

In general, the Competition Lead(s) will consider the repaired robot configuration to be the same
as the original robot configuration if they are satisfied that the repairs have not provided an
unfair advantage to the team going forward. The Competition Lead(s) may also decide that
some past missions may count while others do not.

For example, if the team has performed manipulation missions, then breaks the robot’s arm and
is unable to repair it:


15
     ●​ The team can keep their previous mission scores by satisfying the Competition Lead(s)
        that the robot would not score higher in subsequent tests in its broken configuration than
        its original configuration. This may be achieved by:
             ○​ Keeping the broken arm on the robot, securing it as necessary in a shape that it
                 was originally able to take. The team may adjust the shape of the arm between
                 subsequent missions as long as the Competition Lead(s) are satisfied that those
                 positions are ones that the original robot configuration could have achieved by
                 itself without physical human intervention.
             ○​ Replacing the broken arm with something of similar size, shape, and weight, and
                 satisfying the Competition Lead(s) that it affects the robot in a similar way to the
                 broken arm.
     ●​ If the Competition Lead(s) decide that the repaired robot configuration would score
        higher in subsequent tests than the original robot configuration, the Competition Lead(s)
        shall decide which previously performed tests can be counted, using the following
        guidelines.
             ○​ Mission(s) that the Competition Lead(s) decide are unlikely to have been affected
                 by the arm can be counted. For example, sensor tests (with sensors that are not
                 on the arm, not blocked by the arm, or are otherwise still working).
             ○​ Mission(s) that the Competition Lead(s) decide the repaired robot configuration is
                 likely to outperform the original robot configuration can be counted. For example,
                 mobility tests where the arm was not used to assist in mobility (e.g. by pushing
                 against the ground or changing the center of gravity).
             ○​ Mission(s) where the Competition Lead(s) decide that the repaired robot
                 configuration is not likely to outperform the original robot configuration, or are
                 unsure about, can not be counted. For example, manipulation tests.

NOTE: This rule also covers the complete replacement of the robot (which we consider to be
equivalent to replacing so many parts that none of the old parts are part of the robot). If the new
robot is similar in performance to the old robot prior to it becoming damaged, and otherwise
satisfies the criteria above regarding “no unfair advantage” to the satisfaction of the Competition
Lead(s), this is allowed.


Setting
Some tests may be modified, to increase difficulty. Generally, the initial form of a test will be the
easiest.

Results are only compared for tests performed with the same setting. Tests with multiple
settings are considered separately for the purpose of scoring. For the purpose of the
competition, teams who attempt a harder setting should also attempt the test at an easier
setting.

Each morning of the preliminaries, teams will have an opportunity to attempt the test at the easy
setting before it is made more difficult. The timing of the change depends on scheduling and


16
team capabilities and is at the discretion of the organizers. This allows for additional challenges
for the competitors, without taking up extraneous floor space during competition time.


Session
Each trial day is broken up into multiple sessions, typically two or three, with breaks for meals
and lane inspections in the middle. These sessions are further broken up into 10 minute time
slots.


Starting Position
Where appropriate, a specific location will be indicated in a bay or lane as the appropriate
location for a robot to begin a mission. If in a lane, the robot will be required to begin its mission
within physical contact of the wall at the end of the lane. If, by the start of mission time, it has
rolled away from this starting position, it will be required to return to this position before
commencing its mission. This may be done via teleoperation, or by hand (if it is running
autonomously and has been programmed to avoid touching walls under its own power).


Stowed Posture
To score the autonomy multiplier for sensing and dexterity related tests, the robot must start its
autonomous behavior from its stowed posture. The goal is to avoid teams teleoperating the
robot and arms/flippers/etc. to almost the right position, then having the robot “autonomously”
perform a trivial movement and gaining autonomy points.

We define a robot’s stowed posture as the posture where all articulated appendages that are
directly used for sensing and dexterity (e.g., arms, legs, flippers, or variable chassis geometries)
contribute the least to the robot’s 3D bounding box, as if it were stowed for transport. This does
not include articulated components, such as wheels, that do not change the robot’s 3D bounding
box as they move.

This posture will vary from robot to robot. In general, this will be the arm folded back or retracted
against the body and sensor head tucked away, flippers folded back, and body in “sit” or “lie
down” mode for legged robots. It is up to the autonomous behavior to un-stow the robot and
perform the task. The Stowed Posture should be photographically documented at the start of
the competition.

For some robots, the act of taking on the stowed posture may result in stability and safety
issues. For example, the robot may be on an uneven surface and rely on flippers to stabilize.
The team may elect to only use a subset of the robot’s articulated appendages to score a
particular point, in which case this rule only applies to those appendages. For example, during
the Keypad Omni test, a team may declare that they will only use the arm to push a key. They
will only need to move the arm into the stowed posture prior to beginning the autonomous
behavior but may keep the flippers where the operator left them. Any points scored by any other


17
articulated appendage pushing a key, such as a flipper, will not benefit from the autonomy
multiplier until that appendage is also moved into its stowed posture.


Team Meeting
A team meeting will consist of at least one mentor per team, along with a student as team
captain, along with the Competition Lead(s). Any safety concerns will be covered (fire, crush,
explosion risks), announcements, etc will be covered. Volunteer Judges will also be recruited,
and the team’s runs scheduled for the following session.


Team Participation Form (TPF)
Supplied by teams as the first step in the qualification process. This supplies competition
organizers basic team information and declares intent to compete.


Team Description Materials (TDMs)
Supplied by the teams, these papers are written and delivered prior to the in-person
competition. Ideally, these will clearly demonstrate the team’s process in preparing for the
competition (e.g. equipment used, design choices made, challenges, etc).

These shall be written with the RMRC open source mind-set. The intent is two-fold: demonstrate
the team’s preparedness for in-person competition, and assist upcoming teams in future
competition. (e.g. A poorly written paper, that can not be understood, about a “perfect robot” will
help future rescue teams less than a comprehensible paper on a “poorly performing robot” that
clearly demonstrates to future rescue teams what pitfalls they should avoid!)

The TDMs will be read and judged prior to the competition, on a variety of factors, and a
multiplier score will be awarded based on these documents. Examples from previous years may
be found at https://rrl-rmc.org .


Test
A test is a combination of an apparatus, procedure, setting, and metric that evaluates the
performance of the robot in a specific capability.

NOTE: Prior to 2025, a Test was defined as “An apparatus, procedure, and metric that
evaluates the performance of the robot in a specific capability.” and that a “test” could have
several settings. For example, a terrain test could have settings of “Horizontal” and “Crossover
slope”. These were considered the same test but scored separately. This caused some
confusion as sometimes people didn’t realize that they were scored separately. From 2025
onward, we will consider a test to also be defined by its setting. Thus, from 2025 onward, a
terrain set to “Horizontal” is now referred to as a different test to the same terrain set to
“Crossover slope”.

At the competition

Inspection
The robots will be inspected by a panel of judges before the start of the competition and at other
times during the challenge to ensure that they meet the constraints described in these rules.
Participants will be asked to explain the operation of their robot in order to verify that
construction and programming of the robot is primarily their own work. Unlike in other Junior
competitions, in RMRC, mentors are allowed to assist in the design and construction of the
robots but the students still need to demonstrate both a working knowledge of the entire robot
system as well as knowledge in at least one specific aspect of the robot’s design, construction
and/or operation.

All tests must be attempted with the robot in a configuration identical to what was initially
inspected to be considered together. For instance, running with an arm for dexterity tests and
then removing it for mobility tests will be treated as if the team entered a new robot. Teams who
wish to make changes to their robot during the competition may request this of the Organizing
Committee (OC) on the understanding that, especially if the change is not due to damage
sustained during competition, this may be considered a completely different robot and reset all
scores achieved up until that point. See the definition of “Robot Configuration” for more details.


Preliminaries
The preliminaries consist of each team running their robot(s) through the test methods multiple
times and collecting points (per robot). Each trial is for a single robot; while teams may wish to
bring multiple robots, they must be aware that this will split their available time slots. Entry into
the finals is decided per robot, so this may reduce a Team’s chances to enter the finals.

Time schedule
Each time slot of 10 minutes consists of 3 minutes preparation time, 5 minutes of run time, and
2 minutes to pack up.

The judge starts the run timer when the team leader declares that the team is ready, or at the
latest after the 3 minutes of preparation time has elapsed. This happens regardless of whether
the team is ready or present. Time slots are not delayed. The run will be stopped 8 minutes after
the start of the time slot (or 5 minutes after the mission started if the team started early).

The testing schedule is determined at the team leader meeting prior to each session in the
following way:
     ●​ Many teams run concurrently in separate test method apparatuses. This maximizes the
        amount of time available for teams to compete, demonstrate their capabilities and score
        points.




22
     ●​ During the team leader meeting, teams take turns nominating their run slots for the
        following day’s preliminary competition and populating the scoreboard with their time slot
        nominations.
     ●​ In random order, teams take turns nominating a timeslot and test for a run.
     ●​ Nominations must conform to the following constraints:
             ○​ Teams can only nominate once in each time slot.
             ○​ The maximum number of nominations for a given time slot depends on the
                number of volunteer judges available.
             ○​ Some apparatuses are shared between tests and thus a team nominating for one
                precludes another team nominating for the other in the same time slot.
     ●​ Nominations end when no team wishes to nominate for any more time slots or there are
        no more timeslots left that satisfy the above constraints.
     ●​ At any time during competition, up to 10 minutes prior to a timeslot, two team leaders
        may approach the OC and request to transfer or swap a timeslot. This will be noted on
        the scoreboard.
     ●​ At any time during the competition, up to 10 minutes prior to a timeslot, a team leader
        may approach the OC and request to swap the time slot to a different test, or add more
        runs in empty slots, as long as the apparatus is available and the above constraints are
        satisfied.
     ●​ Each time slot is 10 minutes and includes 3 minutes for operator setup, 5 minutes of
        operation, and 2 minutes to pack up.
     ●​ Teams will set up at the nearest Operator’s station to their indicated test, with one team
        member acting as the operator of the robot. Once the test time begins, the only team
        members that will be in the area will be the robot operator and the team member needed
        to manage the tether.
     ●​ Due to the potentially congested radio environment and restrictions due to local
        regulations on wireless communications, it is strongly recommended that all teams have
        the ability to run with a communications tether.

Scores
     ●​ The goal is to score as many points as possible in the test methods. In general, you get
        one point for getting from the start zone to the end zone and another point for the way
        back from the end zone to the start zone (and so on). See individual test details for the
        scoring metric(s).
     ●​ At the end of each run, both the judge and operator must sign off on the score reached
        by the judge. Any concerns in scoring are to be raised immediately with the judge and a
        member of the OC.
     ●​ Other team members watching are encouraged to cross-check and verify the scores
        before the judge submits the score on the publicly viewable scoreboard. Scores will be
        tallied and recorded on the scoreboard, with the judge and robot operator signing the
        score submitted, to ensure accuracy throughout the competition. If there is a
        disagreement, do not sign the scoreboard, this denotes that the judges will need to
        revisit this score and make a decision.



23
     ●​ Every team can repeat a test, as often they want, subject to the aforementioned team,
        apparatus, and judge scheduling constraints. Only the best resulting score will be kept
        for the task and team.
     ●​ All scores will be normalized per test method, so that the best team in that test gets 100
        points. This calculation is done after all teams have completed all tests in the preliminary
        round. The other teams get points proportionally. Example: For test method Dexterity 1:
        If team A scored 20 points and team B scored 10; then at the end of the preliminaries
        the score of team A will be set to 100 and the score of team B to 50. This way, for each
        test method the best team gets 100 points.
     ●​ This scoring method balances the difficulty level across differing tests. If a test is so
        difficult that only one team attempts it, the team will receive 100 points. Conversely, to
        score well in an easier test that everyone can do, teams must be close to the best.
     ●​ The sum of all points of all tests of a team is then multiplied by the updated TDP score to
        decide the qualification for the final round.


Finals
At the conclusion of the preliminaries, teams will be notified at a team leader meeting who has
qualified for the finals and what factors went into this decision based on scoring as outlined in
chapter “Preliminaries”. Note that qualification into the finals is per robot, not per team. Only one
robot per team may enter the finals.

Following the announcement of the finals-qualified teams, members from each finals-qualified
team will collaborate to design and build the arena from the lanes used in the preliminaries such
that all competing finalists are satisfied with the layout. OC members will work with teams to
ensure each team´s opinion is equally considered. Once the teams have finished assembling
the final configuration of the maze no further changes may be made without the agreed
consensus of all competing final teams.

Final scores are gained by traversing obstacles and terrains formed from the preliminary lanes,
reconfigured into a single maze. This demonstrates the ability of a robot to get to where it needs
to go to perform its task in a manner more representative of the typical challenges facing a
rescue robot. Points can also be collected by sensor and dexterity tests. This represents the
ability of a robot to perform its task. During the final, points can be collected both autonomously,
where the autonomy multiplier is available, and in teleoperation. Apart from re-attempting for
autonomy, all points can only be collected once per final mission. See “Rules for Each Test” for
more details.

Until the end of the preliminaries, we won’t know how many teams qualify for the finals so we
won’t be able to fix the number and duration of finals runs until then. Depending on the number
of qualifying teams and the amount of time available, each team will have 2 to 4 runs, each run
being around 15 to 30 minutes long. The 1st, 2nd and 3rd places are decided by the
accumulated scores of finals runs, with preliminary scores only used to break ties (score
differences of less than 5% are considered ties).
The procedure for each finals round is as follows:

Team Challenge
A Team Challenge is offered as an optional challenge to finish off the international competition.
Similar to the finals, the tests and tasks are developed and discussed together between the
teams beforehand, and the participants should expect increased difficulty. In the Team
Challenge, different teams are combined by lot, based on the number of teams interested in
participating in the challenge, so that each team has an equal number of robots.

The Team Challenge is an optional, casual activity, as a way for students, mentors, and
organizers alike to have some fun and demonstrate collaborative robot capabilities in a less
structured manner.

Throughout the previous 3 days of the competition, the organizers will solicit suggestions from
teams for games or obstacles that may be played on this last day and a decision is made on the
evening of the 2nd day, after the finalists are announced. This means that teams who did not
qualify for the finals have a day to prepare and practice. The Team Challenge varies from year
to year, based on the interests of the competitors. Unlike the other rounds, robots may be
modified for this challenge. It is recommended that the teams think about a strategy beforehand
how they can help each other with their robots, as in this round, robots may push, carry or pull
each other.

Introduction
There are a total of 18 individual tests available to teams in the preliminaries. Some of these
tests are related and share the same apparatus and bay, but have different settings. Others
share a bay because they share a common element, such as a terrain.

Most of the tests can be navigated with a mobile remote-controlled robot that has at least one
sensor to transmit the environmental data to the operator (driver). The operators do not have
eye contact with the robot. In general the operator is placed facing away from the robot.

The competition is divided into preliminary rounds and a final round. In the preliminary rounds,
teams collect points by performing tests during missions. Teams may repeat any test, utilizing
remaining time slots, to attempt a better score. Where a team performs the same test multiple
times with the same robot, the best score is retained. The collected points of the preliminary
rounds will be normalized, added up, multiplied by the Updated TDM score, and used to
determine the final participants.

For each time slot, the team has 3 minutes to start up the robot and operator station in the
operator booth of the respective test lane. After that, the team gets 5 minutes of mission time. At
the end of the mission time, teams have 2 minutes to pack up and clear the arena.

For lap-based tests, the robot must be in physical contact with the start and the end wall to
complete a lap. For each successfully completed lap, the judge scores one point. The conditions
for successfully completing a lap are described below for each test, as are the conditions for
successfully scoring a point on tests that are not lap based.

The robot must not be touched during its mission, nor moved by anything but its own power
(e.g., a person pulling on the tether is not allowed but the tether is allowed to be secured to the
arena at the start of the mission so that a robot equipped with a motorized tether drum or winch
can choose to wind itself back). If the robot is touched, this counts as a reset, points are set to
zero, and the test is re-started with only the remaining time. At the end of the 5 minute mission
time, the largest contiguous score is taken.

Lap preparations
   ●​ The robot starts on the ground at either end and in any orientation.

Scoring points
   ●​ Moving from any level, to any other level, is worth 1 point.
   ●​ A robot is considered to have moved from one level to another if it moves from only
       being in contact with one level, to only being in contact with the next level, without
       returning to the previous level for at least 5 seconds.
           ○​ This excludes situations where the robot “bounces” up and down as it attempts to
               grip the edge of the step as it climbs, momentarily losing contact with the
               previous level, and situations where the robot momentarily “hangs” on one level
               without losing contact with the previous level.
   ●​ For example:
           ○​ Driving up the stairs, touching each level on the way up, from the ground to the
               top level, is worth 3 points in total.
           ○​ Driving off the top level directly to the ground (without touching the other levels
               on the way down) is worth 1 point.
   ●​ The robot continues scoring points in this manner until the mission time ends.

Autonomy bonus
   ●​ A step up, from all ground contacts on one step to all ground contacts on the next step,
      completed without any operator intervention, scores 4 points, as long as the robot stops
      by itself before any operator intervention. An operator intervention to stop the robot also
      removes the autonomy bonus from all steps since the operator gave the last command.
   ●​ A team may start a step autonomously and, if it fails, take over manual control and
      complete it without the autonomy bonus.
   ●​ Teams may alternate teleoperated and autonomous steps in the same mission.

Keypad Omni




Mission objective
This apparatus tests the robots’ ability to precisely touch and push on objects in different
orientations and locations, without touching things around the desired object.

Preparations
   ●​ Ensure that the scoring computer recognizes the keystrokes and that NUM LOCK is on
      (e.g., by pushing a key and making sure the corresponding number appears).
   ●​ Place the robot on one of the outer corners of the dexterity field, in a location and
      orientation of the team’s choosing as long as it does not touch the beams of the terrain.

Scoring points
Each point may be scored multiple times per mission.
   ●​ The robot may drive anywhere on the dexterity field. Incidental contact with any part of
       the keypad omni (e.g., with a wheel) is fine.
   ●​ A key is considered pushed if the key’s number is registered by the scoring computer.
   ●​ The robot may use any part of itself to push keys (including flippers, wheels, etc.).
   ●​ 3 points are scored for pushing the “5” key on a keypad (marked with yellow tape/paint)
       without pushing any other key on that keypad.
   ●​ Only 1 point is scored if any of the keys “1”, “2”, “3”, “4”, “6”, “7”, “8”, and/or “9” are
       pushed (individually or in combination with the “5” key). See example below for
       clarification.
   ●​ A robot may return to score using the same keypad multiple times, but they must drive
       outside the center part of the dexterity field (so that they no longer touch the beams)
       before returning to the same keypad. In effect, driving outside the center part of the
       dexterity field “locks in” their score up to that point and allows them to score all of the
       keypads again. Failing to do so may *lower* their score (see example below).
   ●​ Example 1:
           ○​ The robot pushes the “5” key on the top keypad, slips, and also pushes the “2”
                key, scoring 1 point.
           ○​ The robot drives a short distance and then immediately pushes only the “5” key
                on the top keypad. They score no additional points as they have stayed in the
                center of the dexterity terrain since they last pushed a key on the top keypad.



37
           ○​ They drive out to a corner of the dexterity field, to the point that they no longer
              touch the beams.
           ○​ They return to the top keypad and push the “5” key (without pushing any other
              key on that keypad), scoring 3 points before the mission ends.
           ○​ Their total for the mission (before multipliers) will be 1 + 3 = 4 points.
     ●​ Example 2:
           ○​ The robot pushes the “5” key on the top keypad, scoring 3 points.
           ○​ The robot drives a short distance and then (possibly while maneuvering to push a
              key on a different keypad) pushes the “2” key on the top keypad. What would
              have been 3 points will now be reduced to 1 point.
           ○​ If they score no other points before the time runs out, their total for the mission
              (before multipliers) will be 1 point.

        NOTE: Only the number keys “1” to “9” count in this test. All other keys on the keypad
        are ignored, regardless of if they are covered or not. It is recommended that the NUM
        LOCK key be protected to avoid accidentally turning off NUM LOCK.

Autonomy bonus
   ●​ If the robot starts in its stowed posture and scores the point(s) with no operator input
      apart from a single command (e.g., a button push or mouse click), a 4x multiplier is
      applied to those point(s). The operator may drive the robot anywhere prior to issuing this
      command.
   ●​ The operator may take over to drive the robot, arm, etc., between point(s), but the robot
      must be in its stowed posture before the next attempt at an autonomy bonus.
   ●​ The operator may take over during an attempt at pushing a key to score points without
      the autonomy bonus.
      NOTE: In this test it is possible for an operator takeover to reduce points. For example, if
      the robot autonomously pushes just the “5” key on the top keypad (scoring 3 x 4 = 12
      points), and then the operator takes over and accidentally pushes the “2” key on the
      same keypad before driving out and returning, the score for the top keypad will be
      reduced to 1 point (as they lose the multiplier and the 3 point score for cleanly pushing
      the “5” key). It is therefore recommended that teams’ autonomous behaviors end with all
      parts of the robot well clear of the keypad that was just pushed.
   ●​ Teams may alternate between teleoperated and autonomous points in the same mission.

Team Challenge
On the final day, just prior to the award ceremony, all teams have the option of participating in
the Team Challenge. See the “Competition Logistics and Procedures” section for more details.
Multiple teams work together in a superteam to solve unique and fun challenges. Depending on
the number of teams who wish to participate and the number of people in each team, it is
suggested that each superteam be made up of between 2 and 4 teams.

While the Team Challenge is offered as an optional, casual challenge, the difficulty of the
obstacles will increase from the challenge of the primaries.

Similar to the finals, the tasks are developed and discussed together between the teams
beforehand.

The Team Challenge varies from year to year, based on the interests of the competitors. Unlike
the other rounds, robots may be modified for this challenge, and full contact between the robots
is allowed, including pushing, carrying or pulling each other.

The assignment of teams to N superteams is performed according to the following algorithm.
   1.​ Teams are ordered according to their score in the preliminaries, after all multipliers.
   2.​ Each of the top N teams are randomly assigned to a superteam.
   3.​ The process is repeated with the next N teams until no teams are left.
   4.​ The Competition Lead(s) may step in to re-balance teams if necessary to ensure that
       each superteam has roughly the same number of people (e.g., if the number of teams is
       not evenly divisible by the number of superteams, or if a superteam happened to end up
       with most of the large or small teams).

Code of Conduct

Spirit of Competition
It is expected that all participants (Competitors and Mentors alike) will respect the aims and
ideals of RoboCup as set out in our mission statement. The Competition Lead(s), Judges, and
other officials will act within the spirit of the event to ensure the challenge is competitive, fair,
educational and fun.

Remember that the aim of this competition is for all Competitors to be competing against the
challenge. In a real disaster situation, if multiple teams show up to help, they should work
together to do the best job possible. This is an open source competition where we aim to all
better ourselves and each other; please share and help each other grow!


Expectations
     ●​ All participants should review the most up-to-date rules of the competition (via
        http://list.oarkit.org/ and mailing list) before the competition, to ensure that everyone is

     ●​ All participants must wear their own badge, or be escorted by admin staff, while in the

     ●​ All participants must wear close-toed shoes and secure any loose items/entanglement
        hazards.
     ●​ All participants should present at the venue early on setup day for Team Leader
        meetings, registration, arena walk through, rules clarifications, schedule updates,
        announcements, cover location-specific safety regulations, radio restrictions, etc.
     ●​ All participants must move safely through the entirety of the RMRC area (no running,
        climbing on equipment, obstructing footpaths, or other activities that could lead to the
        harm of self or others.)
     ●​ Competing teams have priority over tables, bays/lanes, and associated thoroughfares.
     ●​ Only Competition Lead(s), Judges, and Admin staff shall communicate with the Robot
        Pilot in preparation for, and during a competition run.


Competition Lead
     ●​ The Competition Lead(s) will keep time and overall score, without bias to any one
        individual or Team.
     ●​ At least one up-to-date copy of the Rules will be printed and available for clarification.
     ●​ At least one copy of the most recently submitted TDMs will be printed and posted for
        general review.
     ●​ Provide enough score sheets for the Judge’s use.
     ●​ If there is any dispute, the Competition Lead(s) will make the final decision, with the

Penalties
Rules will be enforced at the discretion of Competition Lead(s), Judges, officials, competition
organizers and local law enforcement authorities. These individual(s) will decide if the penalties
will only be levied on one individual, or the entire Team. Separate penalties may apply for
disruption during the finals and/or for teams with no more competition runs.

The Competition Lead(s) may, at any time, question a participant on their familiarity with any
relevant matter of safety, particularly if a dangerous or potentially dangerous situation is brought
to their attention. They may make immediate penalty judgment(s), without warning, if they see
unsafe practices, or severe/repeated violations of the code of conduct.


Strike One
     ●​ A verbal, or written, warning will be issued.


Strike Two
     ●​ Removal from current Session, or removal from the RMRC area for 4 competition hours,
        whichever is longer.
     ●​ Scores from the current Session will be struck from the board.
     ●​ Any unsupervised equipment, or Robots, physically present (e.g. on a table, in a lane,
        in a thoroughfare) will be impounded until the end of the next run.
     ●​ Any unsafe equipment, or Robots, physically present (e.g. on a table, in a lane, in a
        thoroughfare) will be impounded and removed from the field of competition until it has
        been deemed safe to return to the competition.
     ●​ RoboCup reserves the right to revoke an award if fraudulent behavior can be proven
        after the award ceremony takes place.


Strike Three
     ●​ Disqualification from the entire competition.
     ●​ Requested to leave the venue.
     ●​ If it is clear that a Team, or Team member, intentionally violates the code of conduct, the
        person or entire Team may be banned from future participation in RoboCup
        competitions.
